Structure

Structure is the backbone of comedy, involving setups, premises, punchlines, misdirection, callbacks, rule of three, escalation, and more.

Subtopics

Setups

A setup provides the context for the joke. It's the foundation that the punchline plays off of.

Examples:
  1. "Two ants are sitting on a log. One says, 'How's the weather today?'"
  2. "I decided to take up gardening."
  3. "My friend started a band called 1023MB."

Premise & Point of View

The premise is what the joke or bit is about. Point of view is the unique angle you bring to it. The same premise can be told a thousand ways depending on who is talking.

Examples:
  1. Premise: "Dating apps are weird." POV: "It's like a video game where everyone else has DLC and I'm still on the demo."
  2. Premise: "Customer service jobs." POV: "I'm paid to smile at people whose personalities should come with a warning label."
  3. Premise: "Growing older." POV: "My back now predicts the weather more accurately than any app."

Punchlines

A punchline is the final part of the joke, where the humor is delivered, usually in a surprising or unexpected way.

Examples:
  1. Setup: "Why don't skeletons fight each other?" Punchline: "Because they don't have the guts."
  2. Setup: "I took a job at the calendar factory." Punchline: "I got fired for taking a few days off."
  3. Setup: "My phone autocorrects 'love' to 'live'." Punchline: "Which is honestly better advice."

Tags & Toppers

Tags and toppers are extra short jokes that come after the main punchline, building more laughs on the same idea without a full new setup.

Examples:
  1. Main punchline: "I joined a gym once. They called to ask where I'd been."
    Tag: "I said, 'Emotionally? Not ready.'"
    Second tag: "They charged me for that conversation."
  2. Main punchline: "My cooking is so bad, even the smoke alarm starts coughing."
    Tag: "It doesn't beep anymore, it just whispers, 'Again?'"
  3. Main punchline: "My plants keep dying."
    Tag: "At this point I'm just running a hospice for houseplants."

Rule of Three

The Rule of Three is a principle that states things are often funnier in threes. The first two items set up the pattern, and the third breaks or twists it.

Examples:
  1. "I went to the store for eggs, milk, and a sense of purpose."
  2. "She bought three cats, two dogs, and a parrot that only says 'Seriously?'"
  3. "He needed three things to make it through the day: coffee, patience, and noise-cancelling headphones."

Heightening

Heightening is taking an idea and pushing it further each time. The situation escalates from normal to ridiculous while following its own logic.

Examples:
  1. "I‚Äôm bad with money. I bought a coffee. Then a coffee maker. Then a course on how to stop buying coffee. On a payment plan."
  2. "My neighbor is loud. First it was music. Then karaoke. Now it's motivational speeches to his blender."
  3. "I told one white lie on my resume. Now according to LinkedIn I'm a 'global synergy architect' with 14 patents and a yacht."

Exaggeration

Exaggeration involves overstating something to an extreme degree for comedic effect.

Examples:
  1. "I'm so hungry I could eat a horse."
  2. "This bag weighs a ton."
  3. "I've told you a million times."

Incongruity

Incongruity humor arises from combining things that don't normally go together, creating an absurd or surreal situation.

Examples:
  1. "A man walks his pet lobster on a leash in the park."
  2. "She brought a spoon to the board meeting."
  3. "He wore a tuxedo to his dentist appointment."

Juxtaposition

Juxtaposition places two contrasting elements side by side to create humor through their differences, such as big vs. small, serious vs. silly.

Examples:
  1. "He‚Äôs as tall as a tree, but as graceful as a bulldozer."
  2. "Her voice was like thunder, but her personality was as gentle as a kitten."
  3. "The tiny elephant strutted through the room, making everyone laugh."

Delivery

Delivery involves the performance aspect of comedy‚Äîhow a joke is told, including timing, pauses, act-outs, crowd work, and other factors that influence how the joke is received.

Subtopics

Timing

Timing is crucial in comedy, as pauses, emphasis, and delivery speed can greatly influence the effectiveness of a joke.

Examples:
  1. Saying, "Comedy is all about... timing," and holding a long pause before the last word.
  2. Waiting a beat after a serious setup before dropping a silly punchline.
  3. Rushing through an obvious part of the story and slowing down on a tiny, ridiculous detail.

Deadpan

Deadpan delivery is characterized by a deliberate lack of emotion or expression, allowing the humor to come through the contrast between the delivery and the content.

Examples:
  1. Flatly saying, "I‚Äôm not arguing, I‚Äôm just explaining why I‚Äôm right."
  2. Delivering, "I have a degree in sarcasm," with a completely straight face.
  3. "I used to be indecisive. Now I'm not sure," said like a status update, not a joke.

Act-Outs & Characters

Act-outs are when you physically play out a moment in the joke‚Äîvoices, faces, body language. Characters are distinct personas you slip into to tell parts of the story.

Examples:
  1. Imitating a confused GPS voice: "In 300 feet, emotionally break down... then turn left."
  2. Acting out a barista writing your name wrong in slow motion, complete with intense concentration and the big reveal.
  3. Switching between your voice and your mom's voice in an argument, with different posture and tone for each.

Crowd Work

Crowd work is interacting with the audience in the moment‚Äîasking questions, reacting to their answers, and riffing off what you see in the room.

Examples:
  1. Asking, "Anyone here on a first date?" then turning their awkward answers into a playful mini-bit.
  2. Spotting someone on their phone: "Don't worry, my self-esteem was too high anyway."
  3. Reacting to a loud laugh: "That was either joy or a cry for help. Blink twice if it's both."

Shock Value

Shock value uses surprising or intense content to jolt the audience, then releases that tension with a joke. It works best when there's still thought and structure behind it.

Examples:
  1. "My life is going great. Which is exactly what I say right before it catches fire."
  2. "I didn't hit rock bottom. Rock bottom filed a restraining order."
  3. "I try to stay positive. It's the only thing I haven't tested negative for."

Repetition

Repetition involves repeating words, phrases, or actions to emphasize a point or create a comedic rhythm.

Examples:
  1. "I‚Äôm not lazy. I‚Äôm not lazy. I‚Äôm just... very committed to resting."
  2. "He keeps saying, 'I need a break,' and I keep saying, 'You need a plan, not just a break.'"
  3. Repeating a tiny phrase from earlier in the set later in a totally different context so it suddenly becomes funnier.

Tension & Release

Tension and release build up an expectation and then release it, often through a punchline that resolves the built-up tension in an unexpected way.

Examples:
  1. "I used to play piano by ear... but now I use my hands."
  2. "She said she was hungry, so I gave her a map. Now she's searching for food."
  3. "He started a diet, threw out all the junk food, meditated, drank water... then remembered pizza exists."

Physical Comedy

Physical comedy relies on bodily movements, gestures, and facial expressions to create humor, often involving slapstick elements.

Examples:
  1. Slipping on a banana peel only to land in a perfectly orchestrated heroic pose.
  2. Tripping over an invisible obstacle and maintaining a totally serious face.
  3. Doing exaggerated dance moves that suddenly freeze in an awkward, held pose.

Word-Based Comedy

Word-based comedy relies on the clever use of language, including puns, sarcasm, wordplay, irony, hyperbole, paraprosdokian, understatement, and more.

Subtopics

Wordplay

Wordplay involves manipulating language to create humor, often through puns, double meanings, and clever twists of phrase.

Examples:
  1. "Time flies like an arrow. Fruit flies like a banana."
  2. "I wondered why the baseball was getting bigger. Then it hit me."
  3. "Outside of a dog, a book is man's best friend. Inside of a dog, it's too dark to read." - Groucho Marx

Puns are a form of wordplay that exploit multiple meanings of a term or similar-sounding words for humorous effect.

  1. "I‚Äôm reading a book on anti-gravity. It‚Äôs impossible to put down."

Sarcasm uses irony to mock or convey contempt, often saying the opposite of what is meant in a biting manner.

Examples:
  1. "Oh, great! Another email. Exactly what I wanted."
  2. "I absolutely love waiting in long lines. It‚Äôs my favorite sport."

Non-Sequiturs

Non-sequiturs are statements that do not logically follow from the previous statement, creating absurd or surreal humor.

Examples:
  1. "I bought a new fridge. It started singing opera."
  2. "She opened her umbrella inside, and the toaster began to sing."
  3. "He decided to knit a sweater while riding a unicycle."

Irony

Irony involves saying something that is opposite to what is meant or expected, often highlighting discrepancies between appearance and reality.

Examples:
  1. A fire station burns down.
  2. "The lifeguard who can't swim."
  3. "A pilot afraid of heights."

Hyperbole

Hyperbole is an exaggerated statement not meant to be taken literally, used to emphasize a point or create strong impressions.

  1. "I‚Äôm so hungry I could eat a horse."
  2. "This meeting has lasted for ten years."
  3. "I've told you a million times."

Understatement plays things down, saying less than the situation deserves, which can be funnier than exaggerating it.

Examples:
  1. After a total disaster: "Well, that could have gone slightly better."
  2. Looking at a huge bill: "Yeah, that's a little above my budget."
  3. After tripping in front of a crowd: "Graceful as always."

A double entendre is a phrase with two meanings at once‚Äîone obvious, one hidden or suggestive. The joke lives in that double meaning.

Examples:
  1. "I told him to take a hike. He thought I meant a nature walk."
  2. "She said my jokes were 'special.' Still not sure which way she meant it."
  3. "He said I had 'interesting' taste in clothes. That word does a lot of heavy lifting."

Comedy Style

Comedy style defines the overall tone and type of humor, including satire, absurdity, dark humor, surreal humor, self-deprecating humor, roasts, and more.

Subtopics

Satire

Satire uses humor, irony, or exaggeration to criticize or highlight flaws in individuals, organizations, or society.

  1. "Why do we call it a building if it's already built?"

Absurdity relies on illogical, nonsensical, or bizarre elements to create humor through the unexpected and the surreal.

  2. "She decided to bake a cake for her pet fish but it had a gluten allergy."
  3. "He tried to juggle oranges while riding a unicycle and then a bear came and punched him in the shoulder."
