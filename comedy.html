Structure

Structure is the backbone of comedy, involving setups, punchlines, misdirection, callbacks, rule of three, exaggeration, incongruity, and more.

Subtopics

Setups

A setup provides the context for the joke. It's the foundation that the punchline plays off of.

Examples:
  1. "Two ants are sitting on a log. One says, 'How's the weather today?'"
  2. "I decided to take up gardening. But I couldn't get to the root of the problem."
  3. "My friend started a band called 1023MB. They haven't gotten a gig yet."

Punchlines

A punchline is the final part of the joke, where the humor is delivered, usually in a surprising or unexpected way.

Examples:
  1. "Because they don't have the guts."
  2. "Turns out, the weather forecast was just a bunch of hot air."
  3. "In the words of Henny Youngman: 'Take my wife‚Äîplease!'"

Callbacks

A callback is a reference to something mentioned earlier in the routine, bringing it back for a punchline later on.

Examples:
  1. Starting with "I told you I have a split personality," and later saying, "Well, that's me, again."
  2. Opening with "I need three things to survive: coffee, chocolate, and my sarcasm," and later joking, "Looks like I forgot one."
  3. First mentioning "My friend is a terrible cook," and later adding, "That's why we order pizza every time."

Rule of Three

The Rule of Three is a principle that states things are funnier in threes. The first two items set up the pattern, and the third breaks it for comedic effect.

Examples:
  1. "I went to the store for eggs, milk, and a sense of purpose."
  2. "She bought three cats, two dogs, and a parrot that only says 'Hello!'"
  3. "He needed three things to make it through the day: coffee, patience, and a sense of humor."

Exaggeration

Exaggeration involves overstating something to an extreme degree for comedic effect.

Examples:
  1. "I'm so hungry I could eat a horse!"
  2. "This bag weighs a ton."
  3. "I've told you a million times."

Incongruity

Incongruity humor arises from combining things that don't normally go together, creating an absurd or surreal situation.

Examples:
  1. "A man walks his pet lobster on a leash in the park."
  2. "She brought a spoon to the board meeting."
  3. "He wore a tuxedo to his dentist appointment."

Juxtaposition places two contrasting elements side by side to create humor through their differences, such as big vs. small, serious vs. silly.

  2. "Her voice was like thunder, but her personality was as gentle as a kitten."

Tension and release build up an expectation and then release it, often through a punchline that resolves the built-up tension in an unexpected way.

Examples:

Examples:

  3. Doing exaggerated dance moves that suddenly stop in a comical pose.
