Super Food Index

Medicinal Nutrition Database

Apples

For thousands of years, apples (Malus sylvestris) have been valued for their ability to address various medical conditions, including diabetes, fevers, inflammatory disorders, and heart ailments. Modern research has validated these benefits and identified important phytochemicals like phloretin, a natural antibiotic.

Apples are abundant in phenols and polyphenols, which provide antioxidant and chemoprotective effects. These compounds help protect against various cancers, including leukemia and cancers of the colon, lung, breast, liver, and skin.

References:
  • Boyer J, Liu RH. Apple phytochemicals and their health benefits. Nutr J 2004; 3:5.
  • Jung M, Triebel S, et al. Influence of apple polyphenols on inflammatory gene expression. Mol Nutr Food Res 2009.
Apricots

In traditional Chinese medicine, apricots have been used to treat asthma, coughs, and constipation. Apricots are a powerhouse of nutrients, including vitamin C, vitamin K, beta-carotene, thiamine, niacin, and iron.

Japanese researchers have also investigated the fruit's ability to inhibit pathogenic bacteria linked to ulcers and acute gastritis.

References:
  • Enomoto S, Yanaoka K, et al. Inhibitory effects of Japanese apricot on Helicobacter pylori. Eur J Clin Nutr 2010.
Bananas

Bananas are low in calories yet packed with essential nutrients like potassium and manganese. They promote probiotic activity, supporting a healthy gut microbiome. Recent research suggests that bananas may help protect against kidney cancer, particularly in women.

References:
  • Chow J. Probiotics and prebiotics: A brief overview. J Ren Nutr 2002.
  • Rashidkhani B, et al. Fruits, vegetables, and renal cell carcinoma risk. Int J Cancer 2005.
Blueberries

Blueberries are packed with flavonoids and polyphenols which have demonstrated potential to reverse cellular aging. Research indicates they protect brain health, enhance memory, and improve coordination, helping guard against dementia and Alzheimer’s.

References:
  • Joseph JA, et al. Reversing the deleterious effects of aging. Am J Clin Nutr 2005.
  • Krikorian R, et al. Blueberry supplementation improves memory in older adults. J Agric Food Chem 2010.
Broccoli

Broccoli contains powerful phytochemicals—diindolylmethane and isothiocyanate—which enhance the immune system and bolster defense against cancer (particularly breast and prostate). It is also rich in calcium, supporting bone health better than many dairy products.

References:
  • Li Y, Zhang T, Korkaya H, et al. Sulforaphane inhibits breast cancer stem cells. Clin Cancer Res 2010.
Carrots

Carrots are rich in alpha-carotene and lycopene, phytochemicals with strong anti-carcinogenic properties. The belief that carrots improve vision is supported by their high levels of retinoids. They also help lower blood sugar and support insulin production.

References:
  • Coleman AL, et al. Glaucoma risk and the consumption of fruits and vegetables. Am J Ophthalmol 2008.
Garlic

Garlic stands out for its selenium content, offering protection against cancer and free radicals. It helps prevent arterial calcification and lowers blood pressure. It also exhibits potent antibiotic properties, combating colds and stomach viruses.

References:
  • Duda G, et al. Effects of short-term garlic supplementation. Pharmacol Rep 2008.
  • Yeh YY, Liu L. Cholesterol-lowering effect of garlic extracts. J Nutr 2001.
Ginger

Ginger is widely used to relieve nausea associated with pregnancy and chemotherapy. Gingerol, a key oil, acts as a natural sedative and antibiotic. Emerging evidence also highlights its cholesterol-lowering effects and potential in managing diabetes.

References:
  • Ali BH, et al. Phytochemical, pharmacological, and toxicological properties of ginger. Food Chem Toxicol 2008.
Goji Berries

The goji berry is a true superfood, boasting high levels of amino acids, minerals, and lycopene. It protects against age-related vision disorders like glaucoma. Research highlights its neuroprotective and immunomodulatory properties.

References:
  • Amagase H, Sun B, Borek C. Lycium barbarum juice improves in vivo antioxidant biomarkers. Nutr Res 2009.
Green Tea

Green tea is famous for its catechins, powerful antioxidants that help reduce plaque buildup in arteries and protect against DNA damage. Its polyphenols may protect against brain cell death linked to Parkinson’s and Alzheimer’s diseases.

References:
  • Cooper R, Morré DJ, Morré DM. Medicinal benefits of green tea: A review. J Altern Complement Med 2005.
Mushrooms

Mushrooms are powerful immune-boosters known to inhibit tumor growth and combat pathogens. Varieties like Reishi and Shiitake offer antiviral properties effective against influenza and hepatitis B, and mutagenic properties that fight various cancers.

References:
  • Wasser SP. Medicinal mushrooms as antitumor and immunomodulating agents. Appl Microbiol Biotechnol 2002.
Oranges

Oranges are loaded with Vitamin C, a powerful antioxidant that combats free radicals. Hesperidin, a key phytonutrient, supports healthy blood vessels. Vitamin C is validated to reduce arterial plaque and protect against Alzheimer’s and arthritis.

References:
  • Kurowska EM, et al. HDL-cholesterol-raising effect of orange juice. Am J Clin Nutr 2000.
Onions

Onions are rich in quercetin and chromium. For diabetics, the chromium helps cells respond to insulin. Onions also help reduce blood pressure and possess anti-inflammatory properties that ease symptoms of asthma and arthritis.

References:
  • Kook S, Kim GH, Choi K. The antidiabetic effect of onion and garlic in rats. J Med Food 2009.
Peppers (Capsicum)

Capsaicin in peppers acts as a natural pain reliever and neuro-inflammatory blocker. It is linked to weight loss and the stimulation of insulin-producing cells. Yellow peppers are also high in lutein, safeguarding eye health.

References:
  • Mori A, et al. Capsaicin inhibits the growth of prostate cancer cells. Cancer Res 2006.
Tomatoes

Tomatoes are the richest source of lycopene, a potent antioxidant preventing cancers such as prostate and lung cancer. They support cardiovascular health by preventing cholesterol oxidation and contain tryptophan, which promotes better sleep.

References:
  • Bhuvaneswari V, Nagini S. Lycopene: a potential anticancer agent. 2005.
