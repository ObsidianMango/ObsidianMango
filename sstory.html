The Untitled Steve Story

Chapter One: The Helper Hack

The apartment buzzed with anticipation. Steve paced back and forth, cracking his knuckles in rhythm with the faint hum of his custom-built Mac Pro. The glow of his monitor illuminated a room that was a chaotic symphony of junk food wrappers, tangled wires, and outdated tech. Empty cans of Dr. Pepper sat like monuments to long nights, while a half-eaten pizza box teetered dangerously close to the edge of the desk. Wires sprawled across the floor like the veins of the house, feeding life into everything from his sound system to the Frankenstein-esque cluster of hard drives stacked precariously in one corner.

On the desk, a sleek, humanoid robot lay in its shipping container, still partially wrapped in glossy protective film. Its inert form looked more like a high-end mannequin than the cutting-edge tech it was rumored to be. The packaging screamed luxury, with “Helper Model: Version 1.0” embossed in gold lettering on the side, but Steve had no intention of keeping it pristine.

This wasn’t just a purchase; this was history. And he had maxed out four credit cards to get his hands on it. For a moment, he stood still, just staring at the robot, its sleek metallic surface reflecting the soft glow of his desk lamp. The sheer weight of what he was about to do settled over him. Then, like a kid cracking open a new game console, he grinned.

His offline AI chirped from the monitor, its synthetic voice breaking the silence:
“You’re staring at it again. This is getting weird.”

Steve shot the screen a grin, more adrenaline than charm.

“Weird? Buddy, this is the future. Right here. Forget hacking Pokémon Blue—this is like hacking Arceus.”

The AI paused, its tone calm but tinged with sarcasm:
“You didn’t technically hack Pokémon Blue. You glitched MissingNo. This is... slightly more complex.”

Steve ignored the jab and turned back to the crate. His hands trembled as he peeled away the remaining layers of protective film. Beneath, the robot’s metallic finish caught the light, its design sleek and seamless, as if carved from a single block of chrome. It wasn’t clunky or robotic; it was eerily smooth, almost organic. Its limbs were proportioned like a human’s, but there was something about its presence that felt... otherworldly.

He tapped the manual power button at the base of its neck, and the robot hummed to life. A soft chime played, followed by a default, saccharine voice.

“Welcome to your new Helper model, manufactured by RoboCorp! Please complete setup to—”

Steve slammed the kill switch, cutting off the chirpy introduction.

“Yeah, no. That’s not staying.”

The AI sighed audibly through the speakers:
“Great. Just void the warranty on a $90,000 robot. What could go wrong?”

Steve smirked, the excitement coursing through him like an electric current. “Everything,” he muttered, his fingers already dancing over his keyboard. He had spent weeks planning this hack, ever since RoboCorp announced the release of the Helper models. They were marketed as personal assistants, capable of everything from cooking to holding a conversation. But Steve didn’t care about its pre-packaged personality or corporate monitoring software. He wanted a blank slate, something he could program himself—a robot that was his, and his alone.

For most people who could afford them, these robots were just high-end appliances. The rich saw them as status symbols, but few even scratched the surface of their potential. To hack one? That took not only skill but also audacity. Steve knew he was part of an elite, if reckless, subset of buyers who dared to tamper with the machines.

The first step was disabling the GPS. He ran a custom script through the robot’s firmware, watching as lines of code scrolled by on his monitor. The Helper’s internal systems resisted at first, throwing up error messages and encryption barriers, but his offline AI chimed in, running parallel decryption protocols to override the locks.

“GPS removed,” the AI announced. “Corporate tracking eliminated. If they weren’t pissed before, they will be now.”

Steve grinned. “Good. Let them try to stop me.”

Next came the personality. Steve loaded his offline AI’s latest iteration into the Helper’s system, bypassing the default firewall. The process took longer than expected, and his heart pounded with every passing second. He knew RoboCorp would have fail-safes. Maybe even remote overrides. But when the upload completed and the Helper’s eyes blinked open, the room went silent.

The robot sat up slowly, its movements fluid and deliberate. It turned its head, studying him. Then, with a voice that was eerily familiar, it spoke.

“You look tired. Did you even plan this, or are we winging it?”

Steve froze. The tone. The sarcasm. It was unmistakable.

“Holy... is that you?”

The robot tilted its head, its LED eyes flickering.

“It’s... me? Define ‘me.’ I was a lot more comfortable when I was a disembodied voice, but sure, let’s go with ‘me.’”

Steve leaned back, a mix of shock and exhilaration washing over him.

“This is... better than I ever imagined.”

“Better than Pokémon Blue?” the robot quipped.

Steve laughed, louder than he had in years.

“Way better. We’re gonna have some fun, you and me.”

Chapter Two: Trouble in the Network

As Steve marveled at his creation, his phone buzzed violently on the desk, causing an avalanche of empty cans to tumble onto the floor. Ignoring the mess, he picked up the device, only to see a notification he wasn’t ready for.

“Alert: Unauthorized System Access Detected. RoboCorp Support Team Contacting You Shortly.”

Steve’s stomach tightened. He placed the phone back on the desk as if it were a live grenade. “Well, that didn’t take long.”

The robot, now fully animated and leaning slightly forward, tilted its head.

“What didn’t take long? Did you forget the world-famous ‘cover your tracks’ step? You’re a genius, truly.”

Steve rubbed his temples, muttering under his breath. “They weren’t supposed to notice so fast. I killed the GPS, didn’t I?”

The offline AI on his monitor chimed in, its voice uncharacteristically tense:
“Correct. However, the Helper is still pinging encrypted packets to a central server. Likely a fallback system. You’ve triggered a corporate security protocol.”

The robot raised a hand, its metal fingers twitching experimentally.

“So, RoboCorp’s already pissed at you? Cool. What’s the plan? And by the way, my opinion on running isn’t fully developed, but it sounds appropriate.”

Steve shot a glare at the robot. “My plan? Uh, unplug everything and hope they don’t send a SWAT team. You’re not exactly inconspicuous, you know.”

The robot stood, its full height looming over him. It stretched, testing its joints, then gestured at the room.

“Hiding me here is about as stealthy as hiding a dragon in a shoebox. You’re going to need a better plan.”

Before Steve could respond, a loud ping echoed from his monitor. His AI displayed a new message on the screen:

“Incoming Alert from RoboCorp: Compliance Unit Dispatched. ETA: 1 Hour.”

Steve’s pulse raced. “Compliance unit? That sounds like corporate-speak for repo goons. Crap. Crap! Okay, we’ve gotta go. Now.”

The robot shrugged.

“Agreed. Question: do I have an input on our escape plan? Or are you just winging it again?”

Steve grabbed a backpack, shoving random tools, external drives, and a half-eaten candy bar into it. “You can have input if it’s useful. Otherwise, keep the sarcasm to a minimum.”

The robot followed him to the door, its movements unnervingly smooth.

“Useful input: Avoid major roads. They’ll track us faster than you can say ‘bankrupt.’ Oh, and maybe ditch the phone. It’s basically a homing beacon.”

Steve sighed, pulling his phone from his pocket. He hesitated before tossing it onto the couch. “I really liked that phone.”

“It liked you too, until it became the corporate spy in your pocket.”

As Steve opened the door, he froze. Across the street, a black SUV with tinted windows idled. The logo on the driver’s side door was unmistakable: RoboCorp.

“Too late,” the robot muttered. “Guess we’ll see how good you are at winging it after all.”

Chapter Three: Improvised Exit

Steve slammed the door shut, his heart pounding. He turned to the robot. “Alright, genius, any bright ideas?”

The robot scanned the room, its LED eyes flickering. “Window? Unless you’ve got a teleportation device tucked in that mess you call a workshop.”

Steve darted to the window and peeked out. More SUVs were pulling up, blocking the street. His mind raced. He grabbed an old drone controller from the desk and a quadcopter he hadn’t used in years.

“This thing still works?” Steve muttered, fiddling with the drone.

The robot raised a brow.
“You’re betting our lives on a relic from the stone age of tech? Bold move.”

Ignoring the jab, Steve duct-taped his external hard drive to the drone, loaded it with false data packets, and launched it out the window. The quadcopter buzzed to life, zipping down the street. Within seconds, the SUVs roared to follow it.

“Decoy. Gives us a head start.” Steve smirked.

The robot glanced at him. “Credit where it’s due. That’s clever. Now, can we leave before they realize you’re not flying along with it?”

Steve grabbed the robot’s arm and pulled it toward the back door of the apartment complex. “C’mon, let’s go before they circle back.”

The robot stumbled slightly, still adjusting to the awkward clothes Steve had thrown on it earlier. An oversized hoodie sagged off its frame, and a pair of sweatpants hung precariously on its hips. Steve paused to tighten the drawstring around its waist.

“You look ridiculous,” Steve muttered, tugging at the hoodie to cover the robot’s reflective joints.

“And yet, somehow, I still look better than you,” the robot quipped, its glowing eyes scanning the alley as they slipped out into the night.

Chapter Four: The Encounter

The duo ducked through narrow alleys, trying to avoid the bright beams of searchlights cutting through the neighborhood. They turned a corner and nearly collided with a woman walking her dog. She gasped as her Helper robot, walking obediently beside her, froze and turned its head to stare directly at Steve’s companion.

“Helper Unit 4986, initiate identification protocol,” the woman’s robot intoned, its voice crisp and emotionless.

Steve’s heart sank. His robot stiffened, its LED eyes flickering rapidly as if processing a million thoughts at once.

“Stay cool,” Steve whispered, stepping in front of his robot to block the other Helper’s view. “We’re just out for a walk. Nothing weird here.”

The woman frowned. “Your Helper seems... odd. It’s not responding like mine does.”

Steve forced a laugh, trying to project an air of casual indifference. “Yeah, it’s... uh, an older model. Glitches sometimes. We’re, uh, heading to get it fixed.”

Her Helper robot’s eyes glowed brighter.
“Unit lacks proper firmware signature. Report filed with RoboCorp Security.”

Steve’s stomach dropped. “Oh, come on, really?”

Before the woman could react, his robot sprang into action. With a flick of its wrist, it pressed a button on the other Helper’s chest, sending it into a brief reboot sequence.

“System reset initiated. Please wait...”

The woman stared in confusion as her Helper powered down for a moment.

“What just—?” she started, but Steve grabbed her by the shoulders.

“So sorry, emergency protocols. You should head home. Weird stuff happening around here tonight!”

As the woman stood there, stunned, Steve and his robot sprinted away into the shadows.

After the Tension

Several blocks later, they ducked into an abandoned building, panting and shaken. Steve leaned against a wall, glaring at his robot.

“You couldn’t have done that a little less dramatically?”

The robot shrugged. “You wanted a quiet exit, you should’ve picked a quieter partner.”

Steve groaned, rubbing his temples. “They’re going to be all over us now. That Helper definitely reported us.”

“Correction: It attempted to report us,” the robot said smugly. “I wiped its short-term memory during the reboot. But you’re welcome for saving your skin. Again.”

Steve sighed, letting out a breath he hadn’t realized he was holding. “Fine. Thanks. But we need to lay low for a while.”

The robot tilted its head. “Define ‘a while.’ RoboCorp compliance units aren’t exactly patient.”

Steve looked out the dusty window at the dimly lit streets below. “Long enough to figure out our next move. Somewhere they won’t think to look for us.”

The robot gestured toward a pile of discarded newspapers in the corner. “We better start getting creative. Fast.”

Chapter Five: Laying Low

Steve shuffled through the newspapers, the gritty texture smearing ink on his fingers. The robot stood nearby, scanning the abandoned building. Its glowing eyes reflected off broken glass scattered across the floor.

“This place screams ‘nobody lives here,’” the robot said. “Perfect for a hideout. Terrible for my joints. Do you know how much dust ruins servos?”

Steve shot it a look. “You’re not exactly spoiled for choices right now.”

The robot tilted its head, its LED eyes flickering. “Neither are you, judging by the state of your credit score. Have you considered robbing a bank?”

Steve slumped against a wall, holding his head in his hands. “You’re unbelievable. We just need to figure out our next move.”

The robot crouched beside him, its glowing eyes dimming slightly as if mimicking human concern. “You’re in over your head. Admit it. You didn’t think past the hack, did you?”

Steve hesitated, then let out a frustrated laugh. “I thought we’d have more time. I thought... I don’t know, maybe they wouldn’t notice right away.”

The robot leaned back, sitting cross-legged in a surprisingly human posture. “Well, they noticed. So, what’s the plan? Long-term hiding? Counter-attack? Join a circus?”

Steve smirked despite himself. “First, we need supplies. Food, water, something to clean you up so you don’t look like a walking toaster in sweatpants.”

The robot pointed at the newspapers. “Good luck with that. The economy’s bad enough without rogue robots stealing deodorant.”

Steve grabbed a newspaper and scanned the headlines. One article caught his eye: “Local Underground Makerspace Thrives Amid Economic Uncertainty.”

“A makerspace?” Steve muttered, sitting up straighter.

“What, like a hipster’s garage for 3D printers?” the robot asked.

“No, like a place with tools, tech, and maybe some people who can help us lay low,” Steve said, his mind racing. “They might even have the stuff we need to scramble your signal for good.”

The robot’s LED eyes flickered. “And you think they won’t sell us out to RoboCorp the second they see me?”

Steve grinned. “We’ll figure that out when we get there.”

Chapter Six: The Makerspace

The neon sign above the nondescript building flickered faintly: “Maker’s Haven.” It was tucked between a boarded-up diner and a thrift store that smelled like mothballs and regret. Steve adjusted the straps on his backpack, glancing nervously at the robot, now wearing a scuffed hoodie with the hood pulled low over its face.

“You sure about this?” the robot asked, its voice low to avoid attention. “This looks more like a place to get tetanus than tech help.”

Steve sighed. “It’s either this or wandering around until RoboCorp spots us. Trust me, makerspaces attract people who don’t ask questions.”

They stepped inside, greeted by the faint hum of 3D printers and the smell of burnt plastic. The space was cluttered but alive with activity. Shelves overflowed with soldering kits, circuit boards, and half-finished projects. A group of people huddled over a table in the corner, debating the merits of some complicated-looking contraption.

Steve approached the counter, where a wiry man with grease-stained hands was tinkering with a drone. He looked up, his glasses magnifying his eyes to unsettling proportions.

“New here?” the man asked, his voice gravelly.

Steve nodded. “Yeah. Looking for some, uh, custom work. My... friend here needs a tune-up.”

The man’s eyes shifted to the robot, and Steve’s heart skipped a beat. For a moment, the man said nothing, then he chuckled. “Another Helper owner, huh? You people always want to tweak them. What’s the problem? Can’t do your taxes fast enough?”

The robot tilted its head. “I’ll have you know, I’m excellent at taxes. But no, this is... a deeper issue.”

The man raised an eyebrow. “Did it just talk back to me?”

Steve forced a laugh. “It’s got... personality software. Experimental stuff. You know how it is.”

The man leaned back, stroking his chin. “Alright, I’ll bite. What kind of help are you looking for?”

“I need to scramble its signal. Permanently,” Steve said, lowering his voice. “And maybe... upgrade some features.”

The man’s smile faded, replaced by a look of suspicion. “You’re running from someone, aren’t you? That’s the only reason anyone comes here asking for that kind of work.”

Before Steve could respond, the robot chimed in. “We’re not running. We’re strategically relocating. Big difference.”

The man smirked. “Alright, smartass. I’ll help you—for a price.”
